‘Fake Woke’ - An Analysis 08 Feb 2021

Recently I had a couple of friends (good friends and reasonable people, I think) introduce me to the rapper Tom MacDonald through his recent song Fake Woke. In it, MacDonald expresses his frustration with “wokeness” and the way he (and others who his political views) are treated by the media and by society. Immediately upon listening to it I felt a pit in my stomach as I heard echoes of conservative talking points permeate his lyrics and yet it drew me in because I couldn’t exactly verbalize what it was that upset me about it. This is my attempt to remedy that.

I plan on going through the song, line by line, and examining the lyrics and trying to extract intent and meaning from the lyrics and to nail down what exactly it is about MacDonald’s approach that I feel is not only misguided (as I think most conservatives are) but also malicious and possibly dangerous. With everything that has happened in the past year (and has been happening for even longer since the rise of the alt right), I hope that we all recognize the dangers of radicalization. I think that MacDonald (whether radicalized himself or not) is weaponizing the energy he sees in the disaffected sea of Trump supporters to sell records or, possibly, something even more callous and sinister.

Introduction and First Listen

First let’s see the video for the song in question:

On a purely aesthetic level, it seems like a decent mainstream rap song. It sounds like something that Eminem would have put out almost two decades ago by which I mean it was a formula that was wildly successful in the past (with white, angry people who listen to white, angry rappers) so why mess too much with the formula too much, right?

MacDonald is a cute guy with lots of tats and piercings suggesting a deft blend of queer aesthetic with more traditional rapper attributes. In some ways, he reminds me of Milo Yiannopoulos in the way that he seems to appropriate some level of (traditionally) leftist culture to attack (perhaps among other things) the left. His shiny diamond medusa and septum piercings lend him an air of femininity which are juxtaposed against his tattooed face and neck and diamond grill and braids. It is clear that he is steeped in and appeals to the culture of younger people who find joy in bending and breaking gender norms (along with other kinds of nonconformity). I mention his appearance and this aspect particularly because here is where I think the story of MacDonald’s motives begins to come to light. He would have you believe that he is (as most famous people do) young and stylish, but also himself not entirely unwoke.

MacDonald, I believe, would consider himself a centrist. He is not burdened by any one dogma and he is not blind to the Zeitgeist of the world around him. Fucking with gender is cool. Tattoos are cool. Outrage is cool. MacDonald is pretty damn cool. Democrats and Republicans are decidedly not. But what message is MacDonald sending in this message and does that jive with his stated position as a “centrist”? Let’s take a look at his lyrics, line by line, and see what we can say about them.

Lyric Analysis

Right off the bat, MacDonald sets the hook to make us realize why (he thinks) what he has to say matters:

I think it’s crazy I’m the one who they labeled as controversial
And Cardi B is the role model for 12-year-old girls
There’s rappers pushing Xanax at the top of the Billboard
But if I mention race in a song, I’m scared I’ll get killed for it

To give some context to what he is talking about, MacDonald has released several songs in recent times that have received some criticism for their poor handling of serious issues. In one particularly-telling example, the rapper facetiously brags about his status of being a ‘Straight White Male’: “Straight white male // Police pull me over, I L-O-L in their face”, intentionally misrepresenting the problem of unjust policing against queer and BIPOC folk and women. He is denying his privilege by mocking a strawman argument taken straight out of Tucker Carlson’s repertoire. But I will leave the analysis of that song for someone else.

The next two lines provide the first insight that MacDonald is not a disinterested centrist, as he would have you believe. Or at least that’s not the people he is speaking to. “Cardi B” and “rappers pushing Xanax” are his nods to moral decay. Cardi B (certainly it was coincidence that he picked a woman of color instead of, say, Miley Cyrus or Ariana Grande) is poisoning the minds of our daughters and unnamed rappers are poisoning our bodies with drugs.

Finally, MacDonald appeals to one of the greatest fears in the souls of his base: the fear of “getting canceled” or otherwise being oppressed for what he beleives. With these few lines, MacDonald has set the tone of the whole song to be rooted in the reactionary politics you see at any Proud Boys rally.

It’s backwards, it’s getting exponentially dumb
It’s more difficult to get a job than purchase a gun

Here’s a point (and there will be a few of these) where I actually agree with MacDonald. I think it probably is easier to get a gun than a job. But regardless of what he feels personally (he is Canadian, after all), his words can be read in two ways: either the problem is that guns are too readily available or it’s getting too hard to get a job. These two ideas are balanced so carefully in this statement, but they appeal to a large swath of people and play into his argument of being centrist or fair-minded.

Eminem used to gay bash and murder his mom
And now he doesn’t want fans if they voted for Trump

BOOM goes the T-bomb. And with this one line he throws in his lot with other Trump apologists (this song was released LONG after he lost the election and even after the Capitol insurrection, for reference) and shows his hand.

It is a problem that Eminem has done these two things, MacDonald argues. Does he mean that Eminem should still gay-bash or be otherwise edgy? Probably not. He definitely wouldn’t admit to such. But the beauty here is that the crowd who does think that will latch onto that just as readily as they latch onto the “shameful” act of disowning his Trump-supporting fans. A progressive person might say that Eminem has grown up and matured or (more skeptically) followed social trends as we as a society have decided we don’t like homophobia (as much) any more. A person who was consumed with, say, conserving their way of life may not take as kindly to that.

We’re ashamed to be American, you should probably love it
‘Cause you have the right to say it and not get strung up in public
As children, we were taught how to walk and talk
But the system wants adults to sit down and shut up

Here MacDonald begins appeals to nationalism (which I found quite funny since he is from Canada although he may be a citizen now) and evokes images of freedom (free speech, really) before lamenting the so-called silencing of (conservative) voices by social media and the press.

Cancel culture runs the world now, the planet went crazy
Label everything we say as homophobic or racist

In case you didn’t believe me that he was talking about cancel culture, here’s his admission. Poor, oppressed conservatives getting called (out for being) homophobic or racist or just plain shitty.

If you’re white, then you’re privileged, guilty by association
All our childhood heroes got Me-Too’d or they’re rapists

With this MacDonald calls back to his denial of white privilege I mentioned above. Even more astonishing to me is this next line, but again MacDonald manages to say something 90% of the way without actually saying it: punishing rapists and people in power who wield it against those without it is a mistake. The rapper would have you thinking back to those great times watching Kevin Spacey movies and worshipping him. Certainly his years of being a big actor boye overwrites his narcissism and abuse and the fact he wielded his sexuality as a weapon when the cards were down. By complaining about rapists facing consequences for their actions, MacDonald (and his idealized listener) are arguing that rapists deserve to go free if they have added enough to the social fabric.

They never freed the slaves, they realized that they don’t need the chains
They gave us tiny screens, we think we’re free ‘cause we can’t see the cage
They knew that race war would be the game they need to play
For people to pick teams, they use the media to feed the flame

This is a favorite argument from people who are “post-racial” (read: unwilling to put in the work necessary to strive for racial equity and desire to just be past it all): racial divides are synthetic barriers and emphasized by those in power to keep us all controlled. Interestingly this isn’t strictly a right-wing theory (I talked about how I saw it come up in discussions with socialists in my last blog post).

What I think is most appalling in this section, however, is the subtle transition he makes from talking about freeing the slaves to the fact that we are all in invisible cages. Here MacDonald is disarming progressive and racially-sensitive types by taking away a point of pride and instead turning it into a (rather trite, in my opinion) comment about “oooo, screen bad.” Here MacDonald is appealing to that part of every white conservative that asks “if racism is bad, why is acknowledging my race and its impact on those around me good?” And in the end, don’t think about the shackles and lash marks on Black slaves. Instead focus on the digital scars we get from having out tweets removed.

They so fake woke, facts don’t care ‘bout feelings
They know they won’t tell me what to believe in
They so fake woke, same old safe zones
They so fake woke, facts don’t care ‘bout your feelings

Here’s the chorus. Mostly this is filled with buzzwords and blatant rip-offs of conservative talking points. Besides “woke” and “safe zones”, long fodder for the conservatives’ fire of disgust against leftists, he trot’s out the good old Ben Shapiro standard “facts don’t care about your feelings”. Once used as a tactic to argue against acceptance of trans folk, it has since become a rallying cry for right-wingers who want to “bathe in leftist tears”. It’s ironic how often this phrase is coupled with non-sequiturs or unfounded cries of “election fraud!” but that’s another story.

I think it’s crazy all these people screaming facts, but they fake woke
Hate their neighbor ‘cause he wears a mask or he stays home
Has a daughter, but his favorite artist said he slays hoes
Picks her up from school, music slaps on the way home

Censorship’s an issue ‘cause they choose what they erase
There’s a difference between hate speech and speech that you hate
I think Black Lives Matter was the stupidest name
When the system’s screwing everyone exactly the same

The first stanza is essentially just rehashing earlier ideas: moral decay bad. One thing worth noting is this is another moment where MacDonald lets his foot off the gas of his conservative hot-rod and throws in a line about appropriately behaving during a pandemic. Unarguably a noble cause, although in context it reads to me much more like an appeal to the less radical listeners whose faith might have started to waver at his earlier rhetoric and a honest appeal to sensibility.

Here’s the racially-negligent nugget of this block, though: “the system’s screwing everyone exactly the same.” Anyone who hasn’t heard the justification for why the BLM movement uses the messaging it does must be intentionally ignoring things so there’s no point in relitigating that. This statement is fucking bullshit, though. The system does NOT treat us equally. Look at the crime statistics anywhere. Fivethirtyeight just did a piece where they analyzed racial data from arrests in cities across the country. Guess what they found? Hint: not what was in the lyrics.

I just wanna spend Thanksgiving Day with food and my family
Without being accused of celebrating native casualties
We got so divided, it’s black and white and political
Republicans are bigots, libtards if you’re liberal

Pretty boring at this point. Nationalism over acknowledging that we committed genocide against the First Peoples. And an appeal to “centrism” because hey he said that “libtard” was a bad thing too!

There’s riots in our streets, and it’s just getting worse
Y’all screaming, “Defund the police”, y’all are genius for sure
They’re underfunded already, they’re way too busy to work
Order food and call the cops, see what reaches you first

MacDonald’s comments again resort to a strawman version of defunding the police. In many reactionaries minds, calls to defund the police end with that. Then money taken from them just disappears or is spent on murdering billionaires or something. But of course the entire point is that large parts of the jobs we generally use police for could be done by unarmed employees who are trained in helping and de-escalating conflict. But yeah “hehe get burrito faster than cop” sure is a lot more pithy.

Segregation ended, that’s a lie in itself
That was a strategy to make us think they were trying to help
They knew that racism was hot if they designed it to sell
We buy up every single box and divide us ourselves

Reprise: segregation (just like slavery) was really a thing that we all suffered under and still do.

We use violence to get peace and wonder why it isn’t working
That’s like sleeping with a football team to try and be a virgin
Politicians are for sale, and someone always makes the purchase
But you and I cannot afford it, our democracy is worthless

If a man has mental illness, call him crazy, say it silently
When country’s going crazy, we accept it as society
Get sick and take a pill when the side effects get you high
You get addicted like these rappers dying fighting with sobriety

The first lines are worth analyzing. MacDonald confuses the aims of BLM (equity) with peace. Peace is a final goal of course, but peace is not how radical changes get made. There is inertia in an oppressive system and friction is required to overcome that. Don’t @ me, physicists. Besides that, we have more of the same: moral decay, appeal towards centrism (all politicians bad lul), etc.

Censoring the facts turns our children into idiots
They claim it’s for our safety, I’ll tell you what it really is
Removing information that empowers all the citizens
The truth doesn’t damage points of view that are legitimate

The last line is delivered in an incredibly self-assured manner and is obviously supposed to be a catchy takeaway that people can latch onto. In context (e.g. released very shortly after the purge of Twitter and other social media of Trump and thousands of spreaders of hatespeech and lies), however, it is easy to read this as a statement of the legitimacy of the claims that the election was “stolen” by Democrats. And so while a rational person would dispel claims of election fraud as a hoax, this gives conspiracy nuts an outlet and a representative they can call their own.

They’re tryna change amen to a-men and women
How’d we let ‘em make praying a microaggression?
Instead of asking God for the strength to keep winning
We cheat to get ahead, and then we ask Him for forgiveness

Blah blah, appeals to religion and “moral fiber” yet again. For the record, I think the congressman who said “A-men… and women!” was also dumb but he certainly wasn’t trying to take away the word “amen” or eliminate religion and is, in fact, a preacher who was trying to weasel in a quick dad joke.

Feminism used to be the most righteous of fights
But these days it feels like they secretly hate guys
I don’t trust anyone who bleeds for a week and don’t die
I’m just kidding, but everything else that I said is right

At this point we’ve all been beat over the head with MacDonald’s pandering to the right. We get it: moral decay, America, cancel culture, “centrism”. So now he puts more effort into crafting his message and so I will put more effort into analyzing it.

We begin by, on the one hand, acknowledging the benefits of feminism and, on the other hand, noting that its time of glory is behind us. In one line he appealed to the “center”, denied that right-wingers are anti-women, but also attempted to defang every feminist critic who might call them sexist. “They secretly hate guys,” he suggests. Echoes back to the concept that “free-thinker” reactionaries are being oppressed by the secret woke elite who want to eliminate men entirely.

At this point, nearly at the end of the song save for a final round of the chorus, MacDonald throws out one more bomb: blatant, hateful sexism. Shock, horror, mouths agape, liberals and leftists with their sensitive dispositions all owned beyond belief. But of course our intrepid hero isn’t actually sexist, so he walks it back right before underscoring how much he believes in “everything else [he] said”. But he didn’t not say it. He knows that that phrase is catchy and has spread among idiot teens and cancerous adults for years. That line embodies political incorrectness while also suggesting that while misogyny isn’t okay, it’s not not funny. In fact, sometimes it can be fun.

Conclusion

So I think what we’ve seen here is that MacDonald’s claim to political “centrism” (whatever that actually means) is questionable at best. I will go on the record right now and claim that the form of centrism that MacDonald is advocating for is, at best, negligent and callous and at worst actively harmful. But it’s a common enough idea that it has gained significant traction among disillusioned right-wingers who think that just because they think both Democrats and Republicans are stodgy, greedy, and corrupt that that makes them “free-thinkers” and, dare I say, “centrist.

But I believe that Fake Woke is more a rallying cry for disaffected (primarily white and conservative) people to push back even harder against progress and equality and to empower them by providing them with confident, successful role models who espouse these views. Tom MacDonald is a lightning rod for the the alt-right. For racists and sexists and misogynists who choose to believe (whether they are right or not) that everyone who drops their dogwhistles believes similarly. MacDonald is just Trump repackaged. He is an outsider. He hates everyone. He hates things that racists, sexists, and misogynists hate and loves what he loves, but he is allowed to move through society because he doesn’t say things explicitly.

Maybe it’s just a callous grab for money and notoriety. Certainly that’s much of what the Trump presidency was. Who knows what MacDonald’s true motives are. But after taking the time to listen to his music and analyze his lyrics I can say that I think he’s not really what I would call a good person. Not that he’d care. But I do.
